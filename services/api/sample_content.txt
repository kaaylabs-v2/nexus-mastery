Strategic Decision Making: Core Frameworks

Module 1: Data-Driven Judgment

Data-driven judgment is the ability to make decisions grounded in evidence rather than intuition alone.
Effective leaders combine quantitative data with qualitative insights to form balanced conclusions.

Key principles:
- Always identify what data exists before forming a hypothesis
- Distinguish between correlation and causation
- Acknowledge data limitations and gaps
- Use structured frameworks (decision matrices, weighted scoring) to organize evidence
- Validate assumptions with at least two independent data sources

Common pitfalls:
- Confirmation bias: seeking data that supports a pre-existing conclusion
- Anchoring: over-relying on the first piece of information encountered
- Analysis paralysis: waiting for perfect data when sufficient data exists
- Gut-feel override: dismissing data that contradicts intuition

Module 2: Risk Assessment

Risk assessment involves systematically identifying, analyzing, and evaluating potential outcomes.
The goal is not to eliminate risk but to make informed tradeoffs.

Framework: IMPACT Analysis
- Identify all stakeholders affected
- Map potential outcomes (best case, worst case, most likely)
- Probability estimation using available evidence
- Assess reversibility — can this decision be undone?
- Consider second-order effects
- Timeline implications — when do consequences manifest?

Module 3: Stakeholder Impact Analysis

Every strategic decision affects multiple stakeholders differently. Effective leaders map these impacts
before committing to a course of action.

Stakeholder mapping steps:
1. List all affected parties (direct and indirect)
2. Assess each stakeholder's power, interest, and influence
3. Identify alignment and misalignment between stakeholder goals
4. Anticipate objections and prepare responses
5. Design communication strategies tailored to each group

The Distributed Team Challenge:
When teams span multiple time zones and cultures, stakeholder alignment requires extra attention to:
- Asynchronous communication norms
- Cultural differences in decision-making styles
- Power dynamics that emerge from proximity to leadership
- Information asymmetry between co-located and remote team members

Module 4: Show Your Work

Externalizing your reasoning process is critical for building trust and catching errors.
When you "show your work," you:
- Make implicit assumptions explicit
- Allow others to identify gaps in your logic
- Create a record that can be reviewed and improved
- Build credibility through transparency

Practice techniques:
- Before meetings, write out your reasoning chain in bullet points
- Use "I believe X because Y" statements
- When challenged, respond with evidence rather than authority
- Ask others to articulate their reasoning too

Module 5: Alternative Generation

Before committing to a solution, generate at least three alternatives. This prevents:
- Premature commitment to the first viable option
- Solution fixation — falling in love with your idea
- Missing creative approaches that combine elements from multiple options

The "What If" technique:
- What if we had unlimited budget?
- What if we had to deliver in half the time?
- What if our main assumption is wrong?
- What if we approached this from the customer's perspective?
- What if we did nothing?
